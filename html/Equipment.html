Equipment: You're telling me this costs money?

Throughout your campaign, you will encounter various weapons, armor, and magical items that aid you throughout your campaign. But your character has to start somewhere, and hopefully they aren't empty handed. Each piece of equipment comes with their own stats and modifiers to assist you on your adventure.

Character Sheet Check!

When you first make your character, most of the equipment you will receive comes from your class and background. When you choose a background from the Player's Handbook, it will come with extra proficiencies and equipment so make sure you take those and write them down. Another thing to take note of before you start reading this section is your Armor Class. This will be largely determined by your armor, with some armors also adding your Dexterity modifier. For the most part, write down all of the equipment you received into the box labelled equipment as well as any money you have. Money in DND is counted in Copper pieces (CP), Silver Pieces (SP), Electrum Pieces (EP), Gold Piece (GP), and Platinum Pieces (PP). Conversion Rates: 1 SP = 10 CP, 1 EP = 50 CP, 1 GP = 100 GP, 1 PP = 1000 CP.

Weapons: For swinging at things

There are several ways to injure a person, and many rules behind these methods. In general, when you attack something, there are two different rolls: the attack roll and the damage roll. To begin with, you have to determine whether or not you actually hit with your weapon. The attack roll is a d20 roll that adds the appropriate modifier and proficiency if you are proficient in that weapon. That number is contested against the target's Armor Class (more on that later). Unless they have the Finesse property, Melee weapons use the Strength modifier and ranged weapons use the Dexterity modifier. Once it is determined that you hit the target, a damage roll ensues. The damage roll is determined by the number in the damage column of your weapon. You will add the appropriate modifier to your damage roll as well, but NOT your proficiency. There are several keywords to understand if you are to utilize your weapon properly.

Martial vs Simple Weapons: To be succint, simple weapons do not require as much training as martial weapons do. Certain classes allow you to be proficient in all simple or all martial weapons, so this is the main distinction that matters in the scope of playing DND.

Damage: The damage you deal to an enemy is determined by what weapon you are using. Some enemies are also resistant to a specific damage type (bludgeoning, piercing, etc). On your character sheet is a box labelled Attacks & Spellcasting. In that box you should write down your main weapon as well as the damage/damage type it does. In the box labelled ATK BONUS, you can write down the number you add to your attack rolls.

Melee Properties: These properties are vital to understanding what weapons your class can or can't use, as well as what ability modifier you should use when you roll damage. A Finesse weapon can use either Strength or Dexterity when calculating attacks and damage, but these modifiers must be the same for both rolls. A Versatile weapon can be used with either one or two hands (the number in parentheses is the damage for a two handed attack). Light weapons allow you to equip one in each hand. Heavy weapons are too bulky for small sized creatures to use, meaning they have disadvantage when attacking with a heavy weapon. A Thrown weapon can be thrown as a ranged weapon (see more about ranged weapons below).

Ranged Properties: Ranged weapons may require Ammunition, but it's up to the DM to require you to keep track of it or not. The smaller number in the Range property determines how far you can shoot without disadvantage. If your range exceeds the smaller number, you may still shoot, but it will impose disadvantage. If your range exceeds the larger number you won't be able to hit at all. Weapons with the Loading property can only be fired once a turn, regardless of how many attacks a character has.

Armor: For when someone swings a weapon at you

Armor is a little bit simpler than weapons. The only special property you have to worry about is if your armor imposes a disadvantage to stealth checks or not. Reminder about disadvantage: When rolling with disadvantage, you roll the same dice twice and take the lesser result. Your Armor Class is determined by the number under the Armor Class (AC) section. When you are attacked, the enemy will roll an attack roll against your Armor Class. If that roll meets or exceeds your Armor Class, you will take damage.
